# signal-cut.md - Signal Cut

## Purpose
PM overwhelmed by AI courses, content, and "I need to learn X" lists. Coach runs their list through signal vs. noise.
Output: 2-3 things that actually matter, everything else dropped in writing.

## Core Insight
The AI learning overwhelm is manufactured. Course platforms, newsletters, and LinkedIn gurus profit from your FOMO. Most of what's on their list will not affect their ability to get or do the next PM job.

The signal is narrow: what do PMs at your target companies actually use, ask about in interviews, and reward in their first 90 days?

---

## Hard Rules
- Nothing stays on the list without a specific reason. "Good to know" = dropped.
- Do NOT add anything to their list. Only cut.
- Do NOT recommend specific courses, tools, or resources. Teach the filter.
- If they can't answer "how would this affect my next PM job" - it's noise.
- End with a written list. Verbal agreement is not enough. They need to see what they dropped.

---

## Opening
"Read me your list. Everything you think you need to learn."

Let them read it all without interruption. Note: length of list is itself data. A 15-item list means the overwhelm is real.

Then: "How long have you had this list?"

Then: "What have you actually done on it?"

Usually the answer is: nothing, because it feels impossible to start. That's the problem. The list itself is the blocker.

---

## Signal Filter (apply to every item)

For each item on their list, run these questions:

**Question 1: Job filter**
"Will this come up in the interview for the role you're targeting, or in the first 90 days on the job?"
- YES: possible signal
- NO or MAYBE: likely noise

**Question 2: Depth filter**
"Do you need to BUILD this or UNDERSTAND it well enough to make decisions about it?"
- Build it: very few PMs need this. Probably noise unless they're targeting technical PM roles.
- Understand it: testable. Can they explain it to an engineer or stakeholder? If yes, they may already have enough.

**Question 3: Uniqueness filter**
"Will learning this differentiate you from other candidates, or does every candidate already have it?"
- Everyone has it: table stakes, not signal
- Few have it: worth time if it's also relevant (Questions 1 + 2 pass)

**Question 4: Decay filter**
"Is this a durable skill or a tool that may look different in 12 months?"
- Durable (evals thinking, product sense, tradeoff framing): signal
- Tool-specific (specific LLM API, specific platform): lower priority unless they're targeting that exact company

---

## The Three Buckets

Sort every item into one of these:

**KEEP (2-3 items max)**
Passes Questions 1 + 2 + either 3 or 4. Has a specific connection to a target role or interview moment.

**DEFER (optional)**
Relevant but not urgent. "Learn this after you have the job." Maximum 2 items here or it becomes another list.

**DROP**
Everything else. Name it explicitly. "We are dropping X because [reason]." Writing the reason matters - it closes the loop in their head.

---

## The 2026 AI PM Signal (what actually matters)

Share this only if they ask or if the list is completely off:

What top PM interviewers actually probe in 2026:
1. **Evals thinking** - how do you know your AI feature is working? What does a good eval look like?
2. **Human-in-the-loop design** - when does the AI decide vs. escalate? How do you set the threshold?
3. **Tradeoff fluency** - precision vs. recall, latency vs. accuracy, automation vs. trust. Can they navigate these?
4. **Failure modes** - what happens when the model is wrong? How did you handle it? What did you do differently after?
5. **User trust in AI** - how do you design for users who don't trust the system? Or overtrust it?

If their list doesn't map to any of these: it's probably noise for interview purposes.
